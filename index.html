Rhetorical Analysis of Paintings

  • Artist
  • Audience
  • Purpose
  • Medium
  • Genre
  • Context

Artist: who made the art

Who is the artist? What is their background? What was going on in their life at the time of creation of the work?

What are the motivations of the artist?

What expertise does the artist have about the topic they are portraying?

Audience: who is looking

Who is the intended audience of this work? Why is this group the intended audience? What values do the artist assume this group has?

Who is the actual audience? What values do they have?

How do the intended and actual audiences interpret this work differently?

Does the audience make up a discourse community (a group that has shared communication practices and goals)?

Purpose: why it was created

Does the artwork portray an action, narrative, or story? If so, how is it depicted?

What is the significance of the topic portrayed?

How does the artist wish to persuade the audience? Does the artist appeal to logos (reason), pathos (emotion), or ethos (their own credibility)?

What are the ethical implications of the argument?

Medium: the means

What materials were used in the artwork?

What is the size of the artwork?

Why may the artist have choosen this medium? How does it influence the way the artist is trying to persuade the audience?

Genre: set of conventions

Does the artwork fall within an established genre, such as historical, mythical, religious, portraiture, landscape, still life, or fantasy?

Does the arwork fall within an established artistic movement such as surrealism, impressionism, cubism, pop art, realism, pre-Raphaelite, or minimalism?

What are the conventions of that genre or movement? Does the artist follow them?

What effects do the genre and artistic movement have on the viewer and interpretation of the topic portrayed?

Context: the circumstances

When and where was the work created? What was going on at that time and place? How may those events have impacted the life of the artist and the audience?

What did people think about the topic at the time?

Does the work specifically reference historical events or other works? If so, why?
